How to Calculate How Much Primer You Need

Primer coverage is not one-size-fits-all. Unlike topcoat paint, the amount you need depends heavily on what surface you're priming and which type of primer you're using — get either wrong and you'll either run short mid-job or overpay for primer you didn't need.

The Basic Formula

Measure the total surface area to be primed, multiply by the number of coats, then divide by the coverage rate for your specific surface and primer combination. Porous or absorbent surfaces pull coverage rates down significantly compared to a smooth, previously painted wall.

Why Surface Type Matters

New drywall and previously painted walls in good condition are the most forgiving surfaces — they accept primer evenly and need less of it. Bare wood, masonry, and stained or water-damaged surfaces are more absorbent or require a stronger sealing product, which lowers effective coverage per gallon.

Choosing the Right Primer Type

Water-based (latex) primer works well for most drywall and previously painted surfaces and cleans up easily. Oil-based primer offers better tannin- and stain-blocking on bare wood. Shellac-based primer is the strongest option for permanently sealing water stains, smoke damage, or dark color changes, though it comes with strong fumes and requires solvent cleanup.

One Coat or Two?

One coat is enough for most new drywall or previously painted surfaces. Bare wood, severe stains, or a dramatic color change from dark to light usually call for two coats to get full, even sealing before you apply your topcoat.

Priming Before You Paint

Once you know how much primer you need, use our Paint Calculator to estimate the topcoat as well — priming and painting together is the most reliable way to plan a full room or exterior project without a second trip to the store.
